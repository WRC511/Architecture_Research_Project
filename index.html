The Martin's House

Frank Lloyd Wright's Martin House, completed in 1907, is a masterpiece of Prairie-style architecture located in Buffalo, New York. The complex features a low-profile design with horizontal lines and a front entrance that is not immediately obvious, enhancing privacy for the residents.

Wright's aversion to curtains led him to incorporate nearly 400 art glass windows throughout the complex, serving as light screens to visually connect exterior views with interior spaces while maintaining privacy. The front porch area, with its low awning, was intentionally designed to discourage lingering, further promoting the family's seclusion. Inside the house, Wright created a subtle and understated staircase, reflecting his belief that the upstairs should be a personal, private family area.

Villa Savoye by Le Corbusier

Villa Savoye, designed by Le Corbusier and completed in 1931, is a masterpiece of modern architecture located near Paris, France. The house's elevated design, supported by slender concrete pillars (pilotis), creates a sense of privacy by lifting the main living spaces off the ground.

Le Corbusier's use of long horizontal windows, while providing ample natural light, also allows for controlled views and maintains privacy for the occupants. The first floor, which contains the private spaces such as bedrooms and bathrooms, is designed with an emphasis on functionality and simplicity, further enhancing the sense of seclusion. Additionally, the rooftop garden and terrace offer a private outdoor space for the residents, reclaiming nature while maintaining separation from the surrounding environment.

Unité d'habitation by Le Corbusier

Le Corbusier's Unité d'Habitation, completed in 1952 in Marseille, France, is a pioneering example of modernist residential architecture. The building's apartments are designed to span from one side to the other, with double-height living spaces, reducing the number of corridors to one every three floors and enhancing privacy for residents. Le Corbusier ensured that there was no contact between different apartments, which were soundproofed to guarantee the privacy of each family. The apartments feature loggias with brise-soleil, providing outdoor space while maintaining privacy and controlling sunlight. Additionally, the building's elevated design on pilotis not only creates a striking visual effect but also lifts the main living spaces off the ground, further contributing to the residents' sense of seclusion.

Broadacre City: Privacy, Space, and the Decentralized Urban Vision of Frank Lloyd Wright

Frank Lloyd Wright presented his conceptual urban planning idea for Broadacre City in 1932, questioning the time's centralized and high-density metropolitan settings. Wright suggested a low-density, dispersed community where every family would get at least one acre of land to dwell on, guaranteeing physical and visible seclusion, unlike the grid-based urban model of cities like New York and Chicago.

The idea sprang from the American ethos of self-sufficiency and individuality. Wright dreamed of a world where every person had enough room to live independently, lessening reliance on packed metropolitan areas. Broadacre City pushed automobile-centric architecture, removing the necessity for public transportation hubs that sometimes violated privacy. Roads linking residences, shops, and civic institutions connected buildings that were to be widely spread apart, and zoning rules would segregate industrial, residential, and commercial areas, therefore limiting unwelcome interference into people’s personal spheres.

Although Broadacre City never was developed, Wright's ideas heavily shaped suburban growth in the middle of the 20th century, when the emergence of vast suburban areas reflected elements of his vision.

Balancing Transparency and Privacy: Architectural Design and Surveillance in Higher Education Institutions

Higher education institutions have progressively included monitoring technologies onto their campuses in recent years, consistently raising privacy issues and affecting the academic environment. One such example is the University of Melbourne, under investigation for its monitoring of student activists using surveillance technologies. The Victorian Information Commissioner's (OVIC) investigation into the institution in August 2024 for using Wi-Fi location data and CCTV footage to reprimand students engaged in a pro-Palestine protest brought attention to the conflict between personal privacy rights and university security policies.

Similarly, in the United States, the development of surveillance devices in learning environments has been increasingly notable in modern times. Critics of tools meant to improve safety and track academic success have attacked their compromise of student autonomy and privacy. Although meant to be protective, these technologies have begged moral considerations regarding the degree of surveillance, and how it affects the learning process for students.

Reimagining Architectural Privacy: A Topological Framework for Spatial Design

In 2006, at the Bartlett School of Graduate Studies, University College London, Michail Georgiou presented a master's thesis titled "Architectural Privacy: A Topological Approach to Relational Design Problems". His work investigates the abstract quality of privacy within architectural spaces, focusing on how enclosed, boundary-defined areas can be analyzed and designed to achieve desired privacy levels.

Georgiou evaluated architectural proposals using a programming environment built from the Processing programming language. This instrument creates conceptual topological diagrams showing the connections between locations depending on given degrees of privacy. Both analytical and synthetic tools, these diagrams let architects record current spatial layouts and test new ideas, fit for certain socio-cultural settings.

The work stresses that privacy is a dynamic topological aspect of space rather than a fixed quality. Georgiou's method provides a complex view of architectural design by grouping places based on their ability to control privacy, therefore transcending the conventional binary division of spaces as either public or private.

The Oculus, New York City

The Oculus is a striking piece of contemporary architecture, with its soaring, wing-like exterior that evokes the image of a bird in flight. Its translucent, white structure creates a dynamic interplay of light and shadow, captivating viewers from the outside.

Inside, the vast open space is flooded with natural light from a dramatic skylight that underscores the building's modern minimalism. The design seamlessly connects the sculptural exterior to a serene, functional interior, symbolizing renewal and the promise of a vibrant urban future.

Grand Central Train Station, New York City

Grand Central Terminal, completed in 1913, is a masterpiece of Beaux-Arts architecture with its iconic facade and grand interior spaces. The terminal's design incorporates numerous security cameras strategically placed throughout the building, including in stairwells, platforms, and the main concourse, allowing for comprehensive surveillance of the bustling crowds. In 2010, hundreds of MTA subway security cameras in Grand Central were directly linked to the NYPD's security operation center, enabling real-time monitoring and potentially raising privacy concerns for travelers. The terminal's open layout, featuring wide passageways and a vast main concourse, facilitates crowd movement but also allows for easier visual monitoring of passengers. Additionally, the installation of high-resolution digital displays throughout the terminal, while primarily used for providing train information, could potentially be utilized for facial recognition or other surveillance purposes.

College Park Airport, College Park, Maryland

College Park Airport, established in August 1909, is the world's oldest continuously operating airport located in College Park, Maryland. Initially created as a training site for the United States Army Signal Corps, it played a crucial role in early aviation history. The airport's original design was simple, consisting of an open field for takeoffs and landings, basic hangars for aircraft storage, and rudimentary facilities. In its early years, the airport lacked modern security measures or privacy considerations, allowing free movement of people and aircraft. The site witnessed numerous aviation firsts, including the first military pilot to solo in a government airplane and the first woman to fly in a powered aircraft in the United States. Over the years, College Park Airport has evolved while maintaining its historical significance, and in 1977, it was added to the National Register of Historic Places.

Marrakech Menara Airport in Morocco

Marrakech Menara Airport’s architecture is defined by its expansive, open design that creates a fluid and welcoming environment throughout its terminal spaces. Floor-to-ceiling glass and airy concourses invite abundant natural light, accentuating the sense of openness.

This open layout is not only aesthetically pleasing but also facilitates effective surveillance by providing clear, unobstructed views of public areas. In this way, the design balances a modern, inviting atmosphere with practical security measures that ensure the safety of all travelers.

A Historical Analysis of Privacy's Influence on Architectural Design

Nowadays in our digital-enveloped society, architectural privacy has become a topic that describes our digital infrastructure and raises large-scale concerns on how much unwanted and non consensual exposure we have on the internet.
However, for this project, our team has taken an interest in exploring the concept of privacy as it relates to the physical world. Specifically, we aim to answer: how are design decisions (and solutions) influenced by evolving notions of the need for privacy in different types of physical spaces within society over time?

We Investigated Privacy In 3 Areas of Architecture

Domestic Privacy:
To analyze how the design of homes (interior layouts, facades, and landscaping) has evolved in response to changing notions of privacy from the late 1700s to the present day. We will look at the impact of social class, technological advancements (e.g., the introduction of electricity and modern plumbing), and cultural shifts on residential architecture.

Societal Privacy:
To investigate the role of privacy in the design of public and civic buildings, such as museums, government structures, and educational institutions, with a particular focus on the period starting with the Industrial Revolution.

Transportation Privacy:
To explore how infringements on privacy, driven by security concerns and technological surveillance, have influenced the design of transportation hubs like airports and train stations from the 20th century to today.

Our Research Approach

Literature Review:
We will conduct secondary research using existing scholarly articles, books, and architectural publications related to the history of privacy and its influence on architectural design. Key themes will be identified.

Case Studies:
We will choose case studies of specific buildings and spaces as examples of the evolving relationship between privacy and architecture. These case studies will be selected to represent different historical periods, building types, and geographical locations. We will interview a property manager to gain insight into privacy features in modern architecture.

Archival Research:
We will explore architectural archives, scholarly sources, and visual materials to gather primary source data on the design and use of buildings across the three focus areas.
